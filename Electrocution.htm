Electrocution
Useful Information

Protection of rescuers and bystanders is of primary concern.

Cardiac arrest is the most common cause of death in lightening and electrocution.

Assume cardiac arrest unless consciousness and breathing have been verified.

Frequently blunt trauma injuries result from falls after loss of consciousness, being "thrown" from the current source and by forceful muscle contraction. Therefore, expect other injuries.

Electricity causes muscles to contract and hands may not be able to release the current source, resulting in prolonged exposure.

Electric current follows a path through the body and may injure all the tissue / organs along this path. The skin injury or burn wound itself may be minor, but underlying tissue damage may be severe, and patient may have multiple wounds representing the entrance and exit of the current.

Thermal bums may be suffered in lightening strikes when the patient's clothes are ignited or when rain or sweat on the skin is turned suddenly to steam, or when metal objects worn by the patient become superheated.

In minor lightening injuries, victims typically appear stunned, and have problems with short-term memory, hearing and vision (not life threatening).
