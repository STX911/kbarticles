Suicidal Callers
Useful Information

Suicide is the intentional killing of one's self. The most common cause is an underlying mental disorder which include depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, alcoholism and drug abuse. Financial difficulties or other undesirable situations play a significant role. Over one million people commit suicide every year, making it the tenth-leading cause of death worldwide. It is a leading cause of death among teenagers and adults under 35. There are an estimated 10 to 20 million non-fatal attempted suicides every year worldwide.[1]

More people die from suicide than from homicide.  (Suicide ranks as the 11th leading cause of death; Homicide ranks 13th.)[2]

Remember thoughts of suicide can be normal but the most important pain-coping resource is the help of a trained mental health professional. A person who feels suicidal should get help, and get it sooner rather than later. [3]

 

HANDLING A CALL FROM A SUICIDAL CALLER[3]

  1. Be yourself. “The right words” are unimportant. If you are concerned, your voice and manner will show it.  Let the person unload despair, ventilate anger. 
  2. Be sympathetic, non-judgmental, patient, calm, accepting.  Avoid arguments, problem solving, advice giving, quick referrals, or belittling.
  3. 95% of all suicidal callers will indicate that their plan is for some date in the future. 

Firearms are the most common method.  Men are more likely to use more violent means and take significant others with them.  Suicide attempts increase with age.

 

You cannot implant the idea of suicide.  Don't be afraid to ask, "Are you having thoughts of suicide?"

 

Avoid using "why?"  Ask instead, "What happened to make you feel this way?"

 

Remember the subject is emotional; expressing negative feelings [hopelessness, helplessness, frustration, rage, anger, etc.]. Allow them to vent and express themselves.  Help guide the person into a more normal functioning level.

 

Suicide calls provide little opportunity for law enforcement control.

Explore areas of commonality and/or determine what is meaningful [" the hook"]  to bond with the caller and establish trust and a rapport.  Use their name exactly as they gave it to you, communicate empathy, defuse emotions, and buy time.  Do not use rank and titles; personalize the call; use your first name.  Stay on the phone; try not to put them on hold.

 

2nd Party Caller:  Assess the environment; if not safe for the caller (2nd party) offer options to make it so.  Never make suggestions that will place the caller in danger.

 

BEWARE

·         Keep responders updated as to changing behavior or activities

·         Be alert to potential suicide-by-cop situations
