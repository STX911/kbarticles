TR Knowledgebase: Burns

Burns
Useful Information

Types:
  • First degree (Reddened skin)
  • Second degree (Blistering skin)
  • Third degree (Charring/pale white; damage to all skin layers)
  • Fourth degree (Involves underlying fat, muscle, and bone)
Special Problems:

Airway Problems: Often associated with facial burns. May be due to thermal injury, as well as reaction to inhaled smoke or fumes and particles of combustion. 30% of victims with major burns have airway problems and all burn patients are at risk of carbon monoxide poisoning.

Electrical Burns: Wounds may appear minor, but underlying tissue damage may be extensive. There may be several wounds representing both the entrance and exit of the electrical current.

Extensive burns may cause shock.

Chemical Burns:

Certain compounds and some industrial chemicals cause increased heat production and penetration when water is applied. HOWEVER, continuous, copious irrigation with a strong  stream of water will significantly reduce these effects and SHOULD NOT BE DELAYED while looking for neutralizing agents. In general, THE EARLIER THE IRRIGATION, THE BETTER THE OUTCOME.

Pediatric considerations:
Smaller airway places children at greater risk for airway compromise. Burns are the second most common cause of accidental death in children under 5 years of age.
